# Guarantee Types

A strong guarantee removes risk from the buyer and puts it on the seller. Better guarantees outperform worse ones almost always — the cost of fulfillment is much lower than the lift in conversion.

## Why Guarantees Work

When the buyer is uncertain, they default to "no" or "maybe later". A guarantee converts the buyer's question from "what if it doesn't work?" to "what do I have to lose?".

The strongest guarantees push that second question all the way to "literally nothing".

## The Five Types

### 1. Unconditional Guarantee
"Money back, no questions asked, anytime within X days."

- **Pros**: Maximum risk reversal. Easiest to communicate. Most converting.
- **Cons**: Easiest to abuse. Some industries can't offer it (perishable goods, custom work).
- **Best for**: Digital products, SaaS, courses, anything where fulfillment cost is low.

Example: "Try it for 60 days. If you don't love it, email us and we'll refund every penny. No forms, no hoops, no questions."

### 2. Conditional Guarantee
"If you don't get [specific outcome] by [specific time], we'll [specific remedy]."

- **Pros**: Specific, believable, ties the guarantee to the dream outcome.
- **Cons**: The buyer must hit the conditions to claim it (which can feel like fine print).
- **Best for**: Services, coaching, anything with measurable outcomes.

Example: "If you don't book your first 5 clients within 30 days of using the system, we'll work with you for free until you do."

### 3. Service Guarantee (Anti-Refund)
"We won't refund. We'll work with you until you get the result."

- **Pros**: Filters out non-serious buyers, signals confidence.
- **Cons**: Higher friction for risk-averse buyers. Don't use as the only guarantee unless your audience is very confident.
- **Best for**: High-touch services where the seller can actually fix things.

Example: "We don't offer refunds, because we don't quit. If something isn't working, tell us and we'll fix it ourselves."

### 4. Implied Guarantee
"Look at our case studies and judge for yourself."

- **Pros**: No formal guarantee at all, but the social proof IS the guarantee.
- **Cons**: Weaker than an explicit promise. Best as a layer on top of one of the above.
- **Best for**: Established brands with overwhelming social proof.

Example: "1,247 customers. 98% renewal rate. Read 200+ case studies."

### 5. The Anti-Guarantee (Reverse)
"You couldn't pay us to take this back. We're so sure you'll keep it."

- **Pros**: Confidence signal, frame inversion, memorable.
- **Cons**: Risky if not earned. Comes off arrogant if the product is unproven.
- **Best for**: Premium brands, contrarian positioning, late-stage offers.

Example: "We don't offer refunds because in 5 years we've had 4 requests. Three of them changed their minds the next day."

## How to Choose

| Situation | Best Guarantee |
|---|---|
| New brand, low trust | Unconditional |
| High-touch service, measurable outcomes | Conditional |
| Premium positioning, established brand | Anti-Guarantee |
| Already strong proof, want to add risk reversal | Implied + Unconditional |
| Cohort/group program | Conditional with deliverable milestones |

## The Trigger + Remedy Format

Every guarantee should be stated in this format:

**Trigger**: What has to happen (or fail to happen) for the guarantee to activate.
**Remedy**: What you do when it activates.

**Trigger**: "If you don't see X result by Y time."
**Remedy**: "We'll Z."

If you can't fill in both halves clearly, the guarantee isn't ready to use.

## The Layered Approach

Stronger offers stack guarantees. Example:

1. "Try it for 30 days, full refund if you want one for any reason." (unconditional)
2. "AND if you do the work and don't see results, we'll work with you for free for another 30 days." (conditional + service)
3. "AND we have 1,247 customers and a 98% renewal rate." (implied)

Three layers of risk reversal in one beat. Hard to say no.
