# Pacing Rules

How fast to cut. Pacing creates rhythm; rhythm holds attention. Every length has a different cut frequency.

## The Cut Frequency Table

| Length | Avg Cut Every | Max Time on One Shot |
|---|---|---|
| 15s | 1 second | 1.5s |
| 30s | 1-2 seconds | 2.5s |
| 60s | 2 seconds | 3s |
| 90s | 2-3 seconds | 4s |
| 3-5 min | 3-4 seconds | 6s |
| 10+ min | 4-6 seconds | 10s |

The rule: shorter videos cut faster, longer videos breathe more. Both extremes (cutting too fast on long-form, cutting too slow on short-form) lose attention.

## Why Cuts Matter

Each cut is a small reset for the viewer's brain. It re-engages attention. When the brain knows another cut is coming soon, it stays tuned. When the brain settles into a static shot, it starts to wander.

This is why TikTok's algorithm favors fast-cut videos: cuts predict watch time.

## The "1 Cut Per Spoken Sentence" Heuristic

A simple rule for any length: at minimum, one cut per spoken sentence. If a sentence runs 4 seconds, one cut at 2 seconds (mid-sentence) keeps the visual moving.

For short-form, you may need 2-3 cuts per sentence (with multiple visual elements supporting one spoken phrase).

## Cut Types (Used to Justify a Cut)

A cut needs a reason. These are the valid ones:

1. **B-roll cut**: switch from talking head to a visual that illustrates the line
2. **Detail cut**: zoom in on a specific element of the previous shot
3. **Reaction cut**: cut to a face responding (testimonial, customer, etc.)
4. **Text cut**: bring up an on-screen text overlay
5. **Pattern cut**: cut to a visually similar shot (motion match, color match)
6. **Hard cut**: quick black flash or whip pan, used for emphasis
7. **Match cut**: object in shot A becomes object in shot B (advanced)

If you can't justify the cut with one of these, don't cut. Random cuts feel manic.

## Pacing By Beat

Within a single video, pacing is NOT uniform. Different beats need different rhythms.

| Beat | Pacing |
|---|---|
| Hook (Part 1) | FASTEST. 1-2 cuts in 3 seconds. Maximum visual urgency. |
| Problem (Part 2) | FAST. Stay punchy; the avatar is uncomfortable, don't let them rest. |
| Promise (Part 3) | SLOW DOWN. The promise is hopeful and visual; let one strong shot land. |
| Mechanism (Part 4) | MEDIUM. Information density is high; don't overwhelm with cuts. |
| Proof (Part 5) | VARIES. Testimonials can sit on one face for 4-5 seconds. Numbers/data should have visual support. |
| Offer (Part 6) | MEDIUM-FAST. One cut per bonus revealed, plus a stack visual. |
| CTA (Part 7) | SLOWS DOWN. The viewer is making a decision; don't distract with cuts. The button or end card should sit on screen for at least 3 seconds. |

## The Audio-Visual Sync Rule

Every visual change should sync to an audio beat: end of a sentence, emphasis word, music beat, sound effect.

Misaligned cuts (visual changes mid-word, mid-phrase) feel sloppy even if you can't articulate why. Sync = polish.

## The Pacing Audit

After building the shot list, calculate:

```
Total cuts ÷ Total duration = Average seconds per cut
```

Compare against the table at the top. If you're outside the range, redistribute.

Also count the longest single shot. If it exceeds the "Max Time on One Shot" column, find a place to add a cut.

## When To Break the Rules

Two situations where slower pacing wins:

1. **Emotional climax**: a single sustained shot during a powerful testimonial moment can hit harder than fast cuts.
2. **Mechanism explanation**: when teaching the named system, slowing down lets the viewer absorb. Don't cut so fast they miss the point.

The default is the table at the top. The exceptions are conscious choices, not laziness.
