# CTA Design

The Call To Action is the moment of decision. If the rest of the video did its job, the CTA only needs to clear the friction.

## The Single CTA Rule

**One call to action. Not three. Not "or you can also..."**

Multiple CTAs split attention. The viewer's brain freezes on the choice and chooses neither. This is the paradox of choice applied to video.

If you find yourself wanting to offer alternatives ("click here OR email us OR follow us"), pick the one with the highest commercial value and cut the rest.

## CTA Anatomy

A complete CTA has three components, all present:

### 1. The Verb
A specific action word. Not "consider", not "explore", not "check out". Use:
- **Click** (universal web)
- **Tap** (mobile-specific)
- **Go to** (works for any platform)
- **Start** ("Start your free trial")
- **Get** ("Get the playbook")
- **Join** ("Join the next cohort")
- **Book** ("Book a 15-minute call")
- **Apply** (when there's a real screening)

The verb sets the friction expectation. "Click" feels light. "Apply" feels weighty. Match the verb to the actual commitment.

### 2. The Destination
Where they're going. Be specific.

- **Bad**: "the link below"
- **Good**: "the orange button on this page"
- **Bad**: "our website"
- **Good**: "go to lockpayments.com/start"

Specificity reduces friction. Vague destinations make the brain ask "wait, where exactly?" and that hesitation kills the click.

### 3. The Reason
Why now. Often pulled from the urgency element in the offer (Stage 02), if any.

- "...to lock in this month's spot before the cap fills up."
- "...to start collecting on time tomorrow."
- "...because the early-bird pricing closes Friday."

If there's no urgency, use the immediate benefit:
- "...to set up in under 60 seconds."
- "...and have your first invoice running today."

## CTA Repetition

**The CTA should appear three times** in any video over 30 seconds:

1. **In the spoken script**: the closing beat (Part 7)
2. **As on-screen text**: visible when the CTA is spoken
3. **As an end card**: the last 3-5 seconds of the video, sitting still on screen

For shorter formats (15s, 30s), the spoken + end card is enough — there's no room for an extra on-screen text overlay.

## The End Card

The end card is the last thing the viewer sees. It should:
- Be on screen for at least 3 seconds (5 seconds preferred)
- Show the destination clearly (URL or button)
- Show the verb-destination phrase
- Be visually static or near-static (this is the moment of decision; don't distract with motion)

Common end card layouts:
1. **Centered button mockup**: a large button-shaped element with the verb-destination text
2. **URL on solid background**: the URL in large text on a brand color, with the verb above
3. **QR code + URL**: for video that might be played in physical settings
4. **Founder face + button**: founder looking at camera + the button next to them

Pick ONE. Don't combine multiple end card styles.

## CTA in Short-Form (Reels, TikTok)

Vertical short-form has unique CTA constraints:

- The "Link in bio" pattern is acceptable BUT inferior to a direct destination
- If using "link in bio", it must appear ON SCREEN as text, not just spoken
- The CTA must complete BEFORE the video loops (because TikTok loops the video automatically and the loop kills the moment of decision)

## The CTA That's Lower Friction

Reduce friction wherever possible. Each phrase below is friction-reducing:

- "No credit card required"
- "60 seconds to set up"
- "Free trial, cancel anytime"
- "Just one click"
- "No phone call needed"
- "We'll set it up for you"

Add ONE of these to the CTA if it doesn't make the message bloated. It's often the difference between "sounds good but maybe later" and "fine, I'll do it now".

## CTA Audit Checks

Before saving the production package, verify:

| Check | Pass Condition |
|---|---|
| Single action | Exactly one CTA, not multiple |
| Specific verb | Concrete action verb, not vague invitation |
| Specific destination | Named URL, button, or location |
| Repeated 3 times | Spoken + on-screen + end card (for >30s videos) |
| Friction acknowledged | If there's a friction-reducing line, it's included |
| End card is static | The last 3-5 seconds aren't visually busy |

## Output for Stage 05

The CTA design appears in two deliverables:

1. **Shot list**: the final 1-3 shots are the CTA shots, with full spoken/on-screen/visual breakdown
2. **Production brief**: a one-paragraph summary of the CTA strategy (verb + destination + reason + friction-reducer + end card style)

The end card design specifically should be detailed enough that whoever is producing the video knows exactly what to put there, including the URL, verb, button color/style, and any logos.
