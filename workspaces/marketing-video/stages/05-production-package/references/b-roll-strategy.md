# B-Roll Strategy

B-roll is the visual that plays under the spoken line. Done well, it doubles the impact of every word. Done badly, it's distraction.

## The Core Rule

**Every spoken sentence should have visual support.** No "talking head only" segments longer than 5 seconds without something visual happening underneath or alongside.

The viewer's brain processes visual and auditory information in parallel. When the visual reinforces the audio, retention goes up. When there's nothing to look at, the brain wanders.

## The Three Functions of B-Roll

Any single piece of b-roll should serve at least one:

### 1. Show What You Say (Literal)
The visual is exactly what the word describes.
- Word: "spreadsheet"
- B-roll: a screen of a spreadsheet

This is the lowest-effort, highest-clarity option. Use it 60-70% of the time.

### 2. Show the Result (After-State)
The visual is the dream outcome from the word.
- Words: "no more late invoices"
- B-roll: a clean dashboard with all invoices marked PAID

This is the most emotionally resonant. Use it on Promise and Offer beats.

### 3. Show the Avatar (Recognition)
The visual is the person you're talking to or someone like them.
- Words: "if you're a coach with 50 clients..."
- B-roll: a coach in their environment, looking stressed

This is the most identification-triggering. Use it on Hook and Problem beats.

## B-Roll for SaaS / Software Products

For digital products, the b-roll palette is narrower but more controllable:

- **Screen recordings of the actual app** (best — proves the product exists)
- **Screen recordings of the OLD WAY** (spreadsheets, manual processes)
- **Animated UI demos** (when the real app isn't pretty enough)
- **Customer screens / dashboards with data** (proof of usage)
- **Founder talking head** (B-roll cuts AWAY from this, not to it)

**Avoid for SaaS**:
- Generic stock footage of "people in offices"
- Smiling stock people pointing at screens
- Generic graphs with no real data
- Lifestyle B-roll that doesn't match the product

**Rule**: a viewer should be able to identify the product from the b-roll alone. If the b-roll could belong to any SaaS, it's wrong.

## The Show-Don't-Tell Substitution

If the script says something abstract, the b-roll should make it concrete.

| Spoken | Bad B-Roll | Good B-Roll |
|---|---|---|
| "saves time" | clock animation | screen recording: 5 minutes of work compressed to 30 seconds |
| "more customers" | line graph going up | screen of CRM with new leads pinging in |
| "easy to use" | smiling user | screen recording of a 3-click flow |
| "your team will love it" | team high-fiving | Slack thread with team members reacting positively |

The good column is harder to produce but lifts the video by an order of magnitude.

## The Static Shot Limit

**Maximum 5 seconds on any single static visual.** After 5 seconds, the eye stops registering it and the brain starts wandering.

If a shot needs to last longer (e.g., a long testimonial quote), add motion within the shot:
- Slow zoom in (Ken Burns effect)
- Camera dolly
- Animated text appearing
- Cursor moving on a screen recording

Motion within a shot satisfies the "something is changing" requirement without needing a hard cut.

## B-Roll Sourcing

For each shot in the shot list, mark the source:

| Source | Notes |
|---|---|
| **Custom screen recording** | Best for SaaS. Plan ahead: which screens to record. |
| **Custom video** | Best for services / physical products. Most expensive. |
| **Stock footage** | Last resort. Use only if it doesn't scream "stock". |
| **Generated / animated** | Motion graphics, type animations, illustrations. |
| **User-provided** | Customer screen captures, testimonial selfies. |

For SaaS specifically, the production package should include a list of EXACT screens to capture, with the action to perform on each.

## The Visual-Spoken Map

In the shot list (Stage 05 deliverable), each row has both a spoken line and a visual description. The visual should make sense WITHOUT the audio. If muting the video kills understanding, the b-roll is too dependent on voiceover.

This is why every script has `[ON-SCREEN: ...]` markers — the on-screen text is the safety net for muted viewing (Reels and TikTok are watched muted by default).

## Output for Stage 05

For each shot in the shot list, fill in the "Visual / B-Roll" column with:

1. **Function**: Literal / Result / Avatar
2. **Description**: specific enough that an editor or videographer knows what to capture
3. **Source**: where it comes from (screen recording / stock / etc.)

If you can't describe the visual specifically, you don't have a shot yet — you have a placeholder. Mark it `[NEEDS B-ROLL DECISION]` and surface it at the next checkpoint.
