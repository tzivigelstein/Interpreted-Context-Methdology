# Hook Formulas

The first 3 seconds of the video. If the hook fails, nothing else matters. If the hook works, you've earned the next 10 seconds.

## What a Hook Has To Do

In 3 seconds:
1. Stop the scroll (visual + verbal interrupt)
2. Activate a specific emotion (curiosity, fear, recognition, surprise)
3. Promise a payoff (something they'll get if they keep watching)

A good hook does all three. A weak hook does one. A bad hook does none.

## The 10 Patterns

Use these as starting points. Don't pick one — generate 5-10 hook variations using different patterns and let the human pick the winner in the checkpoint.

### Pattern 1: Bold Claim
A specific, surprising, hard-to-believe statement that makes the viewer go "wait, what?".

- "I made $10K in a week selling spreadsheets."
- "We replaced our entire support team with one tool."
- "Our app loads faster than Google."

Works because: curiosity demands proof.

### Pattern 2: Direct Pain Identification
Name the avatar's exact pain. They feel called out and lean in.

- "If you're tired of clients ghosting you after the first session..."
- "You know the feeling when Friday hits and you've barely touched your real work..."
- "Every gym owner I've met has the same problem at 50 members."

Works because: mirroring pain triggers identification.

### Pattern 3: Curiosity Gap
Promise a specific reveal but withhold the answer.

- "There's one number on your dashboard that predicts everything. Most founders ignore it."
- "I'll show you the 3-word change that doubled our signups."
- "After analyzing 200 landing pages, this is the only thing that mattered."

Works because: open loops are uncomfortable; the brain wants closure.

### Pattern 4: Negative Frame
Tell them what NOT to do. Instinctively more interesting than positive instructions.

- "Stop doing your weekly review on Sunday."
- "If you're using Notion for client management, you're losing money. Here's why."
- "Don't hire a designer until you've done this."

Works because: warnings activate loss aversion, which is stronger than gain seeking.

### Pattern 5: Question
A specific, uncomfortable question the viewer can't answer comfortably.

- "Are you sure your last 10 customers know your name?"
- "When was the last time you knew your numbers cold without checking?"
- "What if your product is better than your customers think it is?"

Works because: questions force engagement; the brain answers automatically.

### Pattern 6: Personal Failure
Confess a specific mistake. Vulnerability + specificity = trust.

- "I lost $50K before I figured this out."
- "I quit my last business because I couldn't solve this."
- "I made every mistake on this list before fixing it."

Works because: failure stories build credibility faster than success stories.

### Pattern 7: Contrarian
Take a position against the conventional wisdom in the avatar's space.

- "Everyone says you should niche down. They're wrong."
- "Email marketing isn't dead. It's just being done wrong by everyone."
- "Stop A/B testing. Here's what to do instead."

Works because: contrarian positions promise novelty; the avatar has heard the conventional advice 100 times.

### Pattern 8: Authority + Specificity
Establish credentials with a specific number, then promise insight.

- "After working with 1,000+ founders, here's the one pattern I see."
- "I've reviewed 4,000 landing pages this year. This is what's broken on most of them."
- "10 years of email marketing. 3 lessons I wish I'd learned in year 1."

Works because: specificity signals legitimacy; round numbers signal estimates.

### Pattern 9: Specific Promise
Promise a concrete outcome with a specific timeframe.

- "In 90 seconds I'll show you the 4-step framework I use."
- "By the end of this video you'll know exactly which feature to build next."
- "5 minutes from now, your conversion problem will have a name."

Works because: time-bounded promises feel manageable to commit to.

### Pattern 10: Visual Interrupt + Statement
Lead with a strong visual that doesn't match expectations, paired with a short statement.

- [Visual: founder shredding a stack of resumes] "We don't hire from resumes anymore."
- [Visual: deleting a customer] "We just fired our biggest client. Here's why."
- [Visual: empty product backlog] "We shipped zero features in March. Revenue went up 40%."

Works because: visual + verbal incongruence creates a pattern interrupt.

## Format Fit

Different formats need different hook intensities.

| Format | Hook Intensity | Notes |
|---|---|---|
| Reels / TikTok / Shorts | MAXIMUM | Patterns 1, 4, 7, 10 work best. Cold audience, no patience. |
| Landing page hero | MEDIUM | Visitors are warm. Patterns 2, 3, 9 fit. Less aggression. |
| YouTube pre-roll | HIGH | They're trying to skip. Patterns 1, 4, 8 work. |
| Long-form VSL | MEDIUM | Audience already chose to watch. Patterns 6, 8 work. |
| In-app upgrade prompt | LOW | They're already users. Patterns 2, 5, 9 fit. |

## Generating Variations

Don't pick a pattern and write one hook. Pick 5+ patterns and write one hook each. Then rank.

The pattern is the seed. The hook is the variation. Stage 03's process step 2 explicitly asks for 5-10 variations across at least 5 different patterns. This is to avoid premature commitment.

## The "Would I Stop Scrolling?" Test

Read the hook out loud. Imagine you're scrolling Reels at midnight, half-asleep. Would this make you stop?

If no → cut it.
If kinda → rewrite it stronger.
If yes → keep it as a candidate.

A candidate hook should make the writer slightly uncomfortable. If it feels safe, it's not strong enough.
