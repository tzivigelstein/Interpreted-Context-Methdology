# Competitive Landscape

Find the blue ocean: the position where you are the obvious choice, not just one option among many.

## The Three Competitor Categories

Every product has three kinds of competitors. Most marketers only think about the first.

### 1. Direct Competitors
Same solution, same audience.
- Product A vs. Product B in the same category.
- They show up in the same Google search, the same comparison articles, the same review sites.

**Question to ask**: How are we different from them, in 5 words or fewer?

### 2. Indirect Competitors
Different solution, same outcome.
- A project management SaaS competes with a hired assistant. Different solutions, same outcome (less work for the user).
- A meal kit competes with takeout. Different solution, same outcome (no cooking).

**Question to ask**: When the avatar has $X to solve this, where does that money usually go?

### 3. Status-Quo / DIY Competitor
Doing nothing or rolling their own.
- This is the silent killer. Most prospects don't choose between you and your direct competitors. They choose between you and "I'll figure it out myself."
- DIY can be a spreadsheet, a Notion page, a free tool, or just willpower.

**Question to ask**: What does the avatar do today, before any product gets involved?

## The Blue Ocean Move

You don't need to win against all competitors. You need to win against the one the avatar is actually weighing.

Ask: "Of these three categories, which one is the avatar most likely defaulting to right now?"

- If it's **direct**: position on a clear differentiator (faster, cheaper, simpler, specific to their niche).
- If it's **indirect**: position against the trade-offs of the alternative (a hired assistant is expensive and slow; we're cheap and instant).
- If it's **DIY**: position against the hidden cost of DIY (it works until it doesn't; here's what breaks at scale).

The blue ocean is the unguarded angle. Find which category is the real fight, and put 80% of the messaging there.

## The Differentiation Audit

For your top direct competitor, fill in this table. If you can't answer all five rows, you don't have differentiation yet.

| Their main weakness | | |
| Your unfair advantage | | |

A strong differentiation: "Competitor X is built for enterprises with dedicated ops teams. We're built for solo founders who need this to just work without setup."

A weak differentiation: "We're better."

## The Anti-Position

Sometimes the strongest position is what you REFUSE to do. "We don't have a free tier because we don't want users we can't support." "We don't integrate with X because we think X is a dead-end pattern."

Anti-positioning attracts the people who agree with your principle and repels the people who don't. Both effects are good.

## Output for Stage 01

For the positioning brief:

1. **Direct competitor**: name + 1-line differentiator
2. **Indirect competitor**: name + 1-line trade-off you exploit
3. **DIY/status-quo**: describe what the avatar does today + the hidden cost
4. **The chosen fight**: which of the three is the primary battle, with one sentence why
5. **Optional anti-position**: a stance you take that competitors don't

This becomes the input to Stage 02 (offer) and Stage 03 (mechanism). The mechanism in particular should differentiate you from whichever competitor you chose to fight.
